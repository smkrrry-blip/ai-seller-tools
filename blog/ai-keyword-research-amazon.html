How to Use AI for Amazon Keyword Research: Step-by-Step Guide (2026)

By Shoichi  ·  Updated April 2026  ·  7 min read

Shoichi

Amazon FBA seller based in Japan · 5+ years on JP, US, EU · I only review tools I personally pay for.

The Problem: Traditional Keyword Research Takes Too Long

In 2023, my keyword research process for a new Amazon listing looked like this: open Helium 10 Magnet, generate keywords, manually read through 300–500 results, copy ones that looked relevant, paste into a spreadsheet, open Cerebro, run competitor ASINs, copy more keywords, deduplicate manually, sort by relevance in my head, and finally start writing the listing. Total time: 1.5–2 hours per ASIN. With 5–8 new products per year, that's 10–16 hours just in keyword research.

I started using ChatGPT as a filtering and classification layer in mid-2023, and by late 2023 I'd refined a 6-step workflow that consistently gets me from zero to a final, ready-to-use keyword list in about 20 minutes. Here's exactly what I do.

What You Need Before You Start

  • Helium 10 Platinum (for Magnet and Cerebro access) — get it here
  • ChatGPT — the free version works; GPT-4o gives better results. I use ChatGPT Plus ($20/mo).
  • A specific product you're researching

The 6-Step Workflow

Step 1: Generate Seed Keywords in Helium 10 Magnet

Open Helium 10 Magnet and enter 3–4 seed keywords for your product. For a silicone baking mat, I'd enter: "silicone baking mat", "non-stick baking sheet", "oven liner", "silicone pastry mat".

Run each search and take note of two things before exporting: (1) the total result count — if there are fewer than 500 keywords, your seed terms may be too narrow; and (2) the Word Frequency panel on the right side of Magnet. This shows which words appear most often across your results. For my baking mat research, "non-stick", "reusable", "heat resistant", and "dishwasher safe" all appeared in the top 10 — those became required concepts in my listing.

Don't export yet. Note these word frequency insights separately; you'll need them in Step 6.

Step 2: Export the Top 200 Keywords

In Magnet, apply one filter: minimum 300 monthly searches. This cuts out ultra-low-volume noise while keeping long-tail terms with real traffic potential. Then sort by search volume descending and export the top 200 results to CSV.

Why 200? In my testing, going beyond 200 keywords adds diminishing returns — you get progressively more tangential terms — while 200 fits comfortably in ChatGPT's context window without truncation. If your product is in a broad category (sports equipment, kitchen), you may want to run a second Magnet search with different seed terms and export a second 200, then combine the lists before moving to Step 3.

Step 3: Feed to ChatGPT with a Specific Prompt

Open your CSV, copy the "Keyword" column (just the text, not the volume data — ChatGPT doesn't need numbers to classify intent), and paste it into ChatGPT with this prompt:

You are an Amazon listing specialist. I'm going to give you a list of 200 keywords related to [PRODUCT NAME]. For each keyword, classify the buyer intent as High, Medium, or Low using these definitions:

  • High: The searcher clearly wants to buy this product now. Contains purchase signals like "buy", "best", specific product types, size/quantity variants, or brand-adjacent terms.
  • Medium: The searcher likely wants the product but may also be researching or comparing options.
  • Low: Informational or too generic — the searcher may be researching a topic rather than looking to purchase. Also flag any keywords that are off-category.

Return only a formatted list: [keyword] | [High/Medium/Low] | [one-sentence reason]

Here are the keywords:
[paste your keyword list here]

ChatGPT will return a classified list in about 30–60 seconds. GPT-4o is noticeably more accurate at this task than GPT-3.5 — the reasoning column it produces (the one-sentence explanation) catches nuances like "this term is for commercial bakers, not home cooks" that 3.5 misses.

Step 4: Filter by Buyer Intent

Copy ChatGPT's output into a spreadsheet. Sort by the intent column and keep only High and Medium intent keywords. Delete the Low intent rows entirely — you don't want these anywhere in your listing.

In my silicone baking mat example, the original 200 keywords filtered down to 87 High/Medium intent terms. That's a clean, usable list. Before filtering: 200 keywords, many of which were generic ("baking", "kitchen tool", "oven accessories"). After filtering: 87 keywords that represent real purchase intent.

Quick sanity check: skim the High intent list. If you see keywords that surprise you (a keyword you didn't know about with clear purchase intent), make a note — these are often missed opportunities. I found "macaron baking mat with circles" with 1,800 monthly searches this way, which became a secondary keyword cluster in my listing.

Step 5: Check Competition Score in Cerebro

Take your filtered list back into Helium 10. Open Cerebro and run reverse-ASIN searches on your top 3–5 competitors. Filter the results by your keyword list (paste your list into the "search term" filter field) to see where each of your target keywords falls in the competitive landscape.

Key metrics to check:

  • Cerebro IQ Score > 8: High search volume relative to competitor count — worth targeting
  • Competing Products count: Below 400 is generally attractive for a new listing. Above 800 means you'll need significant review velocity to rank.
  • Organic rank of top competitor: If the #1 result has 2,000+ reviews and is ranked page 1 for your target keyword, ranking there organically will take months of PPC investment.

After this step, mark each keyword as "Primary" (target in title and bullets), "Secondary" (include in bullets or description), or "Backend" (search terms field only). You should end up with 10–15 primary keywords and 30–50 secondary/backend keywords.

Step 6: Build the Keyword List for Your Listing

Run your final keyword list through Helium 10 Frankenstein. Frankenstein processes multi-word phrases into individual word tokens and deduplicates them — so "silicone baking mat non-stick" and "non-stick silicone mat" don't generate redundant word instances. This gives you a master word list where every word earns its place.

Then open Helium 10 Scribbles and build your listing. Scribbles shows a checklist on the right side: as you write your title, bullets, and description, it marks keywords green as you use them. Write naturally first, then check which keywords are still unchecked and weave them in.

One final step: paste your completed title into ChatGPT and ask: "Does this Amazon listing title read naturally to a human customer? Point out any phrases that sound keyword-stuffed or awkward." It catches the remaining mechanical-sounding phrases faster than re-reading it yourself after you've been staring at it for an hour.

Real Example with Numbers

Here's what this workflow produced for my US silicone kitchen tool launch in Q3 2025:

  • Initial Magnet keyword pool: 312 keywords across 4 seed terms
  • After export filter (300+ monthly searches): 200 keywords
  • After ChatGPT intent filtering: 87 High/Medium intent keywords
  • After Cerebro competition check: 14 Primary, 42 Secondary, 31 Backend
  • Total workflow time: 22 minutes
  • Compared to previous process: was taking 2 hours

The listing I built from this workflow ranked on page one for 6 of my 14 primary keywords within 60 days of launch, without aggressive PPC. The keyword "silicone spatula set heat resistant" (4,100 searches/mo) — which I only found because Cerebro surfaced it during competitor research — drove 31% of my organic traffic in month two.
